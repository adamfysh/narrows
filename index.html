What the model produces

Three outputs. Every assumption is adjustable.

Built on UN Comtrade bilateral data. Every parameter (sensitivity scores, vessel rates, dependency percentages) can be substituted with your own inputs.

Output 01

Value at risk

Dollar-denominated exposure by country and commodity under a user-defined chokepoint disruption. Severity and duration are variable. Capacity sensitivity is adjustable per chokepoint.

Example: Malacca 100% / 14 daysJapan: $1.6B iron ore · South Korea: $1.1B · China: $3.8B crude
Output 02

Rerouting penalty

Per-voyage cost overrun by vessel class: extra days at sea, bunker and charter rate uplift, inventory carrying cost. Tagged for different users: underwriter, lender, cargo owner.

Example: Suez closure — VLCC via Cape+14 days · $1.4M voyage overrun · $230k carrying cost per vessel
Output 03

Fleet at risk

Vessels in-window during the disruption, cargo value on water at event onset and portfolio rerouting loss across the fleet. The model delivers the accumulation number.

Example: Hormuz 100% / 30 days28 VLCCs in-window · $3.4B cargo at risk · $28M portfolio overrun

Scenario context

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since February 2026.

Iran began charging over $1 million per vessel to transit the strait. 230 loaded oil tankers are at anchor inside the Gulf. Indonesia's finance minister just floated a Malacca toll in response. Thailand is fast-tracking a $31 billion land bridge.

The question of which economies depend on which corridors and what that dependency costs is no longer theoretical. The figures opposite are model outputs from a simulated Malacca closure — 100% severity, 14 days — based on 2022 UN Comtrade bilateral data. They are structural exposure estimates, not live vessel tracking.
